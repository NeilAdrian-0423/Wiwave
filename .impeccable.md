# Wiwave — Design Context

## Users

Families and housemates sharing the same Wi-Fi. These are **not** tech enthusiasts — they're here to talk to the person in the next room, the kid upstairs, the partner cooking in the kitchen, the cousin who's staying over for the weekend. They don't know what WebRTC or RNNoise are, and they shouldn't have to. They care about three things: it's easy, it's private, it works.

Usage pattern: short bursts throughout the day. A phone picked up from the couch, a tablet on the kitchen counter, a laptop on a bedroom desk. One-handed, often while doing something else. Ambient lighting ranges from a sunny breakfast table to a dark bedroom at midnight.

## Brand Personality

**Calm. Private. Honest.**

A well-made tool that doesn't perform for attention. Feels like something built by a person who cares that the thing *works*, not that it *impresses*. Trust comes from simplicity and restraint — no badges, no testimonials, no "AI-powered" garnish.

The north star: an app a parent could open and use without needing to be shown twice. That's the benchmark for every interaction.

## Aesthetic Direction

**Theme**: Follows the system — `prefers-color-scheme`. Light by day, dark by night. The app adapts to the room, not the other way around.

**Visual tone**: Somewhere between a well-designed reading app and a piece of handheld household hardware. Warm, not cold. Soft edges. Generous spacing. Typography carries the load; decoration appears only when it earns its place.

**The one visible piece of machinery**: the audio meter. It's the app's proof-of-work — it shows that noise is being cleaned in real time. Everything else stays in the background.

### References

**Like**:
- Stationery, paper goods, well-designed household objects.
- Apps that feel like tools (Things 3, Reeder, Bear) — confident typography, restrained palette, no visual noise.
- The feeling of a hardback book jacket: tactile, quiet, chosen rather than assembled.

**Avoid**:
- **Enterprise dashboards**: no KPI cards, no sidebar nav, no dense tables, no stock admin iconography.
- **AI-template look**: no generic rounded cards, no purple/cyan gradients, no neon accents on dark, no gradient text, no Inter-on-dark default.
- **Discord / Slack / Zoom aesthetics**: this isn't a workplace tool; it shouldn't feel like one.
- **Hacker / terminal maximalism**: no CRT glow, no monospace-everywhere, no walkie-talkie cosplay. It's a family tool.

## Design Principles

1. **Soft over sharp.** Warm neutrals, rounded corners that aren't cartoonish, low-contrast surfaces. Nothing clinical.

2. **Quiet confidence.** Trust type and spacing to do the work. If a decoration doesn't change what the user understands or feels, remove it.

3. **Respect the room.** Light mode for daytime is paper-white with warm neutrals; dark mode is deep warm charcoal, not cold blue-black. The palette should feel like it belongs in a home, not an office.

4. **The meter is the machinery.** The DSP meter is the one place we show the tool *working*. It's honest and continuous. No other UI element should compete with it for attention.

5. **One-thumb-friendly, always.** Phone-first. Targets large enough for a thumb held casually. No pinch gestures, no tiny dismiss buttons, no scroll traps. If it requires two hands to operate comfortably, it's wrong.

6. **Words chosen, not generated.** Every label, hint, and status message is specific and human. "disconnected from server" > "Error". "empty" > "0 users online". Plain language is the brand.
